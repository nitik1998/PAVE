% Executive summary (replaces traditional conference abstract).
\noindent\textbf{Executive Summary.}\enspace
This report investigates whether vision--language--action (VLA) models such as
$\pi_0$, $\pi_{0.5}$, and OpenVLA preserve the per-class affordance signal
that their underlying foundation vision encoders (DINOv2, SigLIP-So400m)
carried before end-to-end robot fine-tuning. Through eleven experiments
across seven encoders, four task formulations, and two VLA families, we
find that VLA fine-tuning rotates per-class affordance representations out
of the linearly-readable subspace in a class-asymmetric way ---
\texttt{cut} loses 27 pp of intersection-over-union on $\pi_0$ while
\texttt{contain} loses less than 1 pp --- and that this rotation is
mechanistically localised to the final transformer block. The per-class
magnitude of the rotation numerically predicts the per-class accuracy drop
under linear probing (Spearman $\rho = 0.90$, $p = 0.037$ for $\pi_0$). A
297\,000-parameter MLP adapter on top of the frozen encoder recovers 82\%
of the lost \texttt{cut}-class signal, demonstrating that the information
was rotated rather than deleted. Crucially, a complexity-spectrum analysis
shows the apparent 27 pp deficit collapses to under 3 pp on every binary
part-discrimination protocol that downstream manipulation pipelines
actually use, indicating that the standard multi-class probing protocol
overstates the encoder deficit. All experiments fit on a single 8 GB
consumer GPU. Code, weights, and figures: \url{https://github.com/nitik1998/PAVE}.

\section{Problem Definition}
\label{sec:problem}

The central problem this project investigates is the following: \emph{when a
deployed vision--language--action model fine-tunes its visual encoder
end-to-end on robot trajectory data, does the per-class affordance signal
that its underlying foundation encoder carried before fine-tuning still
exist in the resulting representation, and if not, can it be recovered?}

This question matters because of how modern robot manipulation works.
Robotic manipulation in unstructured human environments has shifted, over
the past decade, from precisely modelled industrial pick-and-place to
vision--language--action (VLA) policies that consume raw RGB observations
and natural-language instructions and output low-level robot actions
\cite{kim2024openvla, black2024pi0}. The visual frontends of these
policies are not trained from scratch. Every recent open-weight VLA --- $\pi_0$
\cite{black2024pi0}, $\pi_{0.5}$ \cite{intelligence2024pi05}, and OpenVLA
\cite{kim2024openvla} --- starts from a public foundation vision encoder
(DINOv2 \cite{oquab2024dinov2}, SigLIP-So400m \cite{zhai2023sigmoid}) and
\textit{fine-tunes that encoder end-to-end} on robot trajectory data.

In parallel, an independent body of literature has established that
part-level affordance perception --- distinguishing handle from blade, rim
from bottom, lever from shaft --- is causally important for manipulation
success \cite{li2025affgrasp, tarad2024, turpin2021gift,
mandi2021graff, kokic2017affordance, fsd2026}. The mechanism by which
these papers extract affordance, in nearly every case, is a linear or
near-linear readout on top of a foundation visual encoder. If the
visual encoder inside a deployed VLA still carries per-class affordance
linearly, the entire affordance-conditioned manipulation literature
applies to it. If it does not, those methods may fail silently when
deployed on top of a fine-tuned VLA.

Despite these two literatures, no published study isolates the effect of
end-to-end VLA fine-tuning on affordance-class structure inside the
encoder. Closing that gap is the technical objective of this project.

\subsection{Concrete sub-questions}

The high-level question above breaks into four concrete sub-questions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.3em, itemsep=0.2em, topsep=0.4em]
\item \textbf{Magnitude.} How much of the per-pixel affordance signal in
the SigLIP-So400m vision tower of $\pi_0$ / $\pi_{0.5}$ / OpenVLA is
preserved after VLA fine-tuning, relative to the standalone SigLIP-So400m
they each started from?
\item \textbf{Structure.} Is the loss class-uniform or class-asymmetric?
If asymmetric, which affordance classes (\texttt{grasp}, \texttt{cut},
\texttt{scoop}, \texttt{contain}, \texttt{support}) are most affected?
\item \textbf{Mechanism.} Where in the encoder does the divergence
concentrate? Is the loss caused by a uniform encoder-wide drift or by
class-selective rotations of specific feature directions?
\item \textbf{Recovery.} Is the lost signal recoverable from the residual
representation by a small intervention (such as a non-linear adapter), or
is it actually deleted from the feature space?
\end{enumerate}

This report answers all four.

\subsection{Why each sub-question matters}

\textbf{Magnitude} matters for practitioners deploying VLAs and expecting
the affordance literature's results to transfer; if the signal is largely
preserved, those results apply.

\textbf{Structure} matters because manipulation tasks are themselves
class-specific. A robot deployment that primarily picks and places (a
\texttt{contain}-class workload) will have very different perception
needs than one that cuts or shears (a \texttt{cut}-class workload). If
the loss is class-asymmetric, the deployment risk is class-specific.

\textbf{Mechanism} matters because it tells us \emph{whether the loss is
fixable}. A uniform drift suggests the encoder simply needs a linear
recalibration. A class-selective rotation in a specific layer suggests a
targeted intervention is possible.

\textbf{Recovery} matters because it determines the practical
intervention. If a small adapter on top of the frozen encoder restores
the signal, deploying teams do not need to retrain expensive
foundation models --- they can attach a tiny head.

\section{Literature Survey}
\label{sec:litsurvey}

This section reviews three lines of prior work that this project builds
on: (i) affordance perception for robot manipulation, including the
foundational pre-2021 literature; (ii) modern vision--language--action
models; and (iii) representation analysis of deep networks.

\subsection{Affordance perception for robot manipulation}

The notion of \emph{affordance} originated in ecological psychology with
Gibson's 1979 framework \cite{gibson1979ecological}, in which an object's
affordances are the action possibilities it offers an agent. The first
operationalisation of affordance for robotics came from Myers, Teo,
Ferm{\"u}ller, and Aloimonos at ICRA 2015 \cite{myers2015umd}, who
introduced the UMD Part Affordance dataset --- a 17-category collection
of household tools with per-pixel labels for seven primitive affordance
verbs (\texttt{grasp}, \texttt{cut}, \texttt{scoop}, \texttt{contain},
\texttt{pound}, \texttt{support}, \texttt{w-grasp}). UMD remains the
standard benchmark for per-pixel affordance segmentation a decade later
and is the dataset on which all probing experiments in this report are
performed.

Building on UMD, Kokic, Stork, Haustein, and Kragic (Humanoids 2017)
\cite{kokic2017affordance} demonstrated task-specific grasping: their
system uses a CNN to segment the object into task-affording sub-regions
and an optimisation-based grasp planner to select grasps consistent with
those sub-regions. Their pipeline outputs human-like grasps for ten tool
classes across five tasks --- this is the earliest end-to-end
demonstration that affordance-aware perception, not just geometric
stability, is necessary for usable grasping. Their experiments
specifically contrast their method against pure force-closure baselines
and report substantially better task-relevant grasps in qualitative
evaluation.

Nagarajan, Feichtenhofer, and Grauman (ICCV 2019)
\cite{nagarajan2019grounded} introduced grounded interaction hotspots
learned from egocentric video, demonstrating that affordance heatmaps
can be discovered from passive observation without manual annotation.
Their work is the conceptual precursor to several modern self-supervised
affordance discovery methods.

GRAFF (Mandikal and Grauman, ICRA 2021) \cite{mandi2021graff} integrated
visual affordance priors into deep reinforcement learning for dexterous
hand manipulation in MuJoCo. Their headline result: training proceeds
3$\times$ faster when the agent's reward is shaped by an
affordance-region prior. GIFT (Turpin \emph{et al.}, RSS 2021)
\cite{turpin2021gift} showed that interaction-aware tool-use affordances
can be discovered through procedurally-generated training in simulation,
without any human annotation, and generalise to novel tools at 71--80\%
success on hammering and sweeping tasks. Both 2021 papers establish that
\emph{affordance perception causally improves manipulation success}, the
premise on which our investigation rests.

More recent work continues this trajectory. Aff-Grasp (ICCV 2025)
\cite{li2025affgrasp} reports +30 pp grasp success vs.~baselines without
affordance. TARAD (RA-L 2024) \cite{tarad2024} integrates VLM-derived
affordances with a 3D diffusion policy and reports +52 pp absolute task
success gain. FSD (ICLR 2026) \cite{fsd2026} achieves 72\% real-world
success vs.~42\% for the strongest non-affordance baseline. The
empirical conclusion, established across nearly a decade of work, is
that affordance is causally important for manipulation. Our work does
not re-derive this; we cite it and use it as the motivation for asking
whether deployed VLAs preserve the signal.

The current state-of-the-art on UMD per-pixel affordance segmentation is
Zhang \emph{et al.} CVPR 2026 \cite{zhang2026umd}, who report 0.670 mIoU
using a dense decoder on top of foundation features. We exceed this with
a 60-second linear probe alone, as Section~\ref{sec:results} reports.

\subsection{Vision--language--action models}

OpenVLA (Kim \emph{et al.}, CoRL 2024) \cite{kim2024openvla} is the
flagship open-weight VLA. It builds on the Prismatic VLM
\cite{karamcheti2024prismatic} which fuses DINOv2 and SigLIP-So400m
visual encoders into a Llama-2-7B language backbone, fine-tuned on the
970,000-trajectory Open X-Embodiment corpus \cite{padalkar2024oxe}.
$\pi_0$ (Black \emph{et al.}, 2024) \cite{black2024pi0} is its
contemporary, using a PaliGemma backbone with a flow-matching action
head. $\pi_{0.5}$ \cite{intelligence2024pi05} is the successor with an
improved training recipe. All three are publicly released as
\verb|safetensors| checkpoints, which makes encoder extraction feasible.
The effect of VLA fine-tuning on the underlying encoder's affordance
representation has not been studied in any of these papers.

\subsection{Probing and representation analysis}

Linear probing as a measurement methodology was popularised by Alain and
Bengio \cite{alain2017probes} for analysing intermediate layers, and
refined by He \emph{et al.} (CVPR 2022) \cite{he2022masked} and Oquab
\emph{et al.} (TMLR 2024) \cite{oquab2024dinov2} for self-supervised
vision encoders. The methodology is now standard: a linear (or
log-linear) classifier on frozen features measures \emph{linearly
readable signal}.

Kornblith, Norouzi, Lee, and Hinton (ICML 2019)
\cite{kornblith2019cka} introduced linear Centered Kernel Alignment
(CKA) for layer-wise representation comparison. CKA is invariant to
orthogonal transformations and isotropic scaling but \emph{not} to
arbitrary affine transformations; this property makes it the appropriate
measurement for comparing two networks of the same architecture, as we
do for standalone vs. post-VLA SigLIP. Our layer-wise CKA analysis in
Section~\ref{sec:results} closely follows Kornblith \emph{et al.}'s
recipe.

\section{Proposed Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

The project's methodology consists of four interlocking measurements
applied to the same set of vision encoders: (1) linear probing for
per-class affordance accuracy, (2) layer-wise CKA for localising the
divergence between standalone and post-VLA encoders, (3) per-class
final-layer feature drift for testing whether the divergence is
class-selective, and (4) a 297\,K-parameter MLP adapter as an active
intervention testing whether the lost signal can be recovered from
the frozen post-VLA representation. This section describes each method,
the intuition behind it, and the theoretical motivation.

\subsection{Method 1 --- Linear Probing}

Given a frozen visual encoder $\phi$ that maps an RGB image
$\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{S \times S \times 3}$ to per-patch features
$\mathbf{F} = \phi(\mathbf{x}) \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times D}$, where
$N = (S/p)^2$ for patch size $p$, we fit a multinomial logistic
regression $W \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times K}$ over all training patches
with $K$ classes. Patch labels are obtained by majority-voting the
per-pixel UMD labels under each $14 \times 14$ patch footprint. At
test time we apply $W$ to compute $K$-channel patch logits, softmax,
bilinear-upsample to image resolution, and reduce to a hard label by
argmax (with background reconstructed as $1 - \max_{c \neq \mathrm{bg}} p_c$).
The probe is fit by \texttt{sklearn.LogisticRegression} with $L_2$
regularisation $C = 1$ and converges in approximately 60 seconds on
CPU.

\subsubsection{Intuition}

A linear probe measures \emph{linearly-readable signal}: the question
"how well-organised is class-$c$ information in the encoder's feature
space such that a single hyperplane can separate class-$c$ patches from
the rest?" If an encoder's features carry class-$c$ information
\emph{at all} (regardless of structure), a non-linear head with
sufficient capacity could read it. A linear probe is more restrictive: it
asks whether the information is in linearly-readable directions. The
critical implication is that a linear probe's verdict is sensitive to
the \emph{rotation} of the feature space, not just its content.

\subsection{Method 2 --- Layer-wise CKA}

Given two encoders of identical architecture (e.g., standalone
SigLIP-So400m and the SigLIP tower extracted from $\pi_0$), we run both
on a fixed image set and collect hidden states
$H^{(\ell)}_A, H^{(\ell)}_B \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$ at every
transformer layer $\ell$. Linear CKA between the two is
\[
\mathrm{CKA}\!\left(H^{(\ell)}_A, H^{(\ell)}_B\right) =
\frac{\| H^{(\ell)\top}_A H^{(\ell)}_B \|_F^2}
     {\| H^{(\ell)\top}_A H^{(\ell)}_A \|_F \cdot \| H^{(\ell)\top}_B H^{(\ell)}_B \|_F}
\]
after centering each $H$ over the patch dimension. A CKA value of 1
means the two layers' feature spaces are equal up to an orthogonal
transformation; a CKA value near 0 means the two are nearly orthogonal.

\subsubsection{Intuition}

CKA tells us \emph{at which depth} two networks of the same architecture
diverge. For our problem, plotting CKA between standalone SigLIP and
each post-VLA SigLIP across layers $\ell = 0, \ldots, 27$ localises
where the VLA fine-tuning has "moved" the encoder. If divergence
concentrates in the final layers, the rotation is happening late; if
divergence accumulates from layer 0, the entire encoder has been
reorganised.

\subsection{Method 3 --- Per-class Final-layer Drift}

For the final transformer layer $\ell = L$, we slice the patch features
by ground-truth label. For class $c$, define
$\mu_c^{(\phi)} = \mathrm{mean}_{i\,:\, y_i = c}\, H^{(L)}_{\phi, i}$ as
the class-mean feature. The per-class \emph{drift} between standalone
and post-VLA encoders is
\[
\mathrm{drift}_c = 1 - \cos\!\left(\mu_c^{(\mathrm{std})}, \mu_c^{(\phi)}\right).
\]
A small drift means the class-$c$ features point in roughly the same
direction in both encoders; a large drift means the class-$c$ features
have rotated.

\subsubsection{Intuition}

Drift is a \emph{class-stratified} measurement. CKA compares
representations across all patches; drift compares only
class-$c$ patches. If certain classes have larger drift than others,
the rotation is class-selective, not uniform. We then test whether
class-selective drift correlates with the per-class IoU drop measured
by linear probing --- a positive correlation is evidence that the
encoder-internal geometry numerically predicts the downstream behavior.

\subsection{Method 4 --- MLP Adapter Recovery (Intervention)}

The final method is an active intervention. We replace the linear
probe with a 2-layer MLP $h: \mathbb{R}^{1152} \to \mathbb{R}^{256} \to
\mathbb{R}^{6}$ with GELU activation, dropout 0.1, and class-balanced
cross-entropy loss. Total parameters: 297\,K. Training: 40 epochs,
AdamW with learning rate $2 \times 10^{-3}$ and weight decay $10^{-4}$,
batch size 8\,192 patches. The encoder is \emph{frozen} throughout.

\subsubsection{Intuition and theory}

The intuition tests a falsifiable hypothesis. If VLA fine-tuning
\emph{deletes} per-class affordance information from the feature space,
no head --- linear or non-linear --- can recover it. If VLA fine-tuning
\emph{rotates} per-class information out of the linearly-readable
subspace but preserves it elsewhere in the feature space, a small
non-linear head should recover most of it. The 297\,K-parameter MLP is
large enough to compensate for low-rank rotations but small enough to
preclude memorisation (the UMD train set has only $345 \times 256 =
88\,320$ patches; a 297\,K-parameter model is data-bound, not
capacity-bound). The success of the recovery is therefore evidence
about the structure of the loss, not an artifact of overfitting.

\section{Baseline Methods}
\label{sec:baselines}

To make the post-VLA encoder measurements interpretable, we compare each
post-VLA encoder against five baselines that frame the answer.

\subsection{DINOv2-base and DINOv2-large}

DINOv2 \cite{oquab2024dinov2} is a self-supervised vision foundation
model from Meta. We probe both \verb|facebook/dinov2-base| at $448^2$
and \verb|facebook/dinov2-large| at $448^2$ and $560^2$. These provide
a vision-only foundation reference unaffected by any
language-instruction or robot-action signal during training. They are
the strongest non-VLA encoders for general-purpose probing; their
performance establishes the upper bound for a foundation encoder
without VLA fine-tuning.

\subsection{Standalone SigLIP-So400m at $224^2$}

This is the \emph{matched} foundation reference. The SigLIP vision
tower inside $\pi_0$, $\pi_{0.5}$, and OpenVLA all started as
SigLIP-So400m at $224^2$ resolution. We probe the publicly-released
\verb|google/siglip-so400m-patch14-224| with the same protocol used on
the post-VLA encoders. This baseline is the single most important
comparison: it is what each post-VLA encoder \emph{was} before VLA
fine-tuning, and the difference between standalone and post-VLA scores
is the direct measurement of what VLA fine-tuning destroyed.

\subsection{SigLIP-base at $256^2$}

A smaller VLM-style encoder \cite{zhai2023sigmoid} probed identically.
Provides a sanity check that the SigLIP family in general carries
affordance signal, distinguishing the SigLIP-So400m result from a
base-SigLIP capacity ceiling.

\subsection{Random projection control}

A random initialisation of the same architecture, untrained. Probing it
yields ~0.18 mIoU, isolating the contribution of the foundation
features. This baseline rules out the alternative explanation "the
linear probe is doing all the work and the foundation features don't
matter."

\subsection{Florence-2 zero-shot grounding}

Florence-2 \cite{xiao2024florence2} is a zero-shot grounding VLM. We
prompt it with the affordance class names and use its returned
bounding-box predictions as the segmentation. This baseline tests
whether modern grounding-trained VLMs can substitute for probing on the
affordance task. The empirical answer in our experiments: no ---
Florence-2 collapses to ~0.16 mIoU on this fine-grained per-pixel
problem.

\subsection{Linear probe vs.\ MLP adapter (head ablation)}

The MLP adapter from Section~\ref{sec:methods} is itself compared
against a linear probe head on the same backbone, controlling for the
backbone and isolating the contribution of the head's non-linearity.
This is what allows the rotation-vs-deletion test to be informative.

\section{Evaluation Methodology}
\label{sec:evaluation}

This section specifies the dataset, train/val/test splits, metrics, and
comparison protocols used to evaluate every method in the report.

\subsection{Dataset}

We use the UMD Part Affordance dataset \cite{myers2015umd}: 17 tool
categories, each with multiple instances and per-pixel affordance
labels. Figure~\ref{fig:umd} shows representative examples.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/umd_examples.png}
\caption{Representative UMD test images alongside ground-truth
affordance labels and predictions from four backbones. Each row is a
distinct tool category (mug, knife, hammer, shovel, scoop). Columns
show RGB, ground-truth affordance map, and predictions from
DINOv2-large, standalone SigLIP-So400m, $\pi_0$ SigLIP, and the
$\pi_0$+adapter intervention. The dataset captures the per-pixel
class structure that all probing experiments measure.}
\label{fig:umd}
\end{figure}

The native UMD taxonomy has seven affordance verbs:
\texttt{\{grasp, cut, scoop, contain, pound, support, w-grasp\}}.
Following \cite{myers2015umd}, we collapse \texttt{grasp+w-grasp} into a
single \texttt{grasp} class and drop \texttt{pound} due to its small
sample size, yielding a 5-class+background taxonomy:
\texttt{\{grasp, cut, scoop, contain, support\}}.

\subsection{Splits}

We use a 500-sample stratified split balanced across categories:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.3em, itemsep=0.0em, topsep=0.2em]
\item Train: 345 images (used to fit linear probes and adapters).
\item Validation: 73 images (used for hyperparameter selection,
including image-resolution sweeps).
\item Test: 75 images (used only for final reported numbers).
\end{itemize}
The split is committed at \verb|data/umd/splits_500/| in the public
repository for reproducibility.

\subsection{Per-class IoU and mean IoU}

For each class $c$, intersection-over-union is
\[
\mathrm{IoU}_c = \frac{|\{i: y_i = c\} \cap \{i: \hat{y}_i = c\}|}
                       {|\{i: y_i = c\} \cup \{i: \hat{y}_i = c\}|}.
\]
Mean IoU (\verb|mIoU|) is the average across foreground classes.
We report per-class IoU on every result so the asymmetric pattern is
visible.

\subsection{Balanced accuracy and AUC for binary tasks}

For binary part-discrimination tasks (\textsc{H9}, \textsc{H10a},
\textsc{H10b}), we report \emph{balanced accuracy}
$\frac{1}{2}(\mathrm{TPR} + \mathrm{TNR})$ and the area under the ROC
curve. Class balancing is applied via \verb|class_weight=balanced| in
the logistic regression to handle the heavy imbalance between
\texttt{cut} and other-foreground patches.

\subsection{Bootstrap confidence intervals}

For the headline per-class IoU numbers we report 95\% bootstrap
confidence intervals with $n_{\mathrm{boot}} = 1000$ resamples at the
image level. Image-level resampling preserves the within-image patch
correlation that pixel-level resampling would destroy.

\subsection{Linear CKA and per-class drift}

Implemented as in Section~\ref{sec:methods}. Numerically stable on
$N = 18\,688$ patches across $D = 1152$ dimensions. We report layer-wise
CKA at all 28 layers (input embedding + 27 transformer blocks) for
each VLA against standalone SigLIP-So400m.

\subsection{Spearman and Pearson correlation}

For the mechanism-predicts-behavior link
(Section~\ref{sec:results}.\ref{sec:mechpredict}), we compute Spearman's
rank correlation $\rho$ and Pearson's $r$ between per-class drift and
per-class IoU drop, with two-tailed $p$-values. Significance is
reported despite the small sample size ($n = 5$ classes per VLA).

\subsection{Complexity-spectrum design}

To localise where on the task-difficulty axis the apparent loss
manifests, we evaluate cut detection under four progressively easier
formulations:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.3em, itemsep=0.1em, topsep=0.2em]
\item \textbf{H2} --- 5-class IoU on full UMD. Hardest. Multi-class
confusion across 17 tool categories.
\item \textbf{H10a} --- Binary "\texttt{cut} vs.\ rest-foreground" on
full UMD foreground patches. Multi-class data, binary readout.
\item \textbf{H10b} --- Binary "\texttt{cut} vs.\ rest" evaluated on
multi-tool composite scenes built by tiling a knife with a
non-\texttt{cut} distractor. Adds visual co-presence.
\item \textbf{H9} --- Binary "handle vs.\ blade" restricted to
knife/scissors/shears/saw images only. Easiest. Single-object
discrimination.
\end{itemize}

The four-formulation spectrum is the central methodological design
that lets us distinguish "VLA encoder has lost cut" from "the H2 metric
overstates the loss".

\section{Experimental Design and Results}
\label{sec:results}

This section presents eight experiments. Sections 6.1--6.5 measure the
core probing-mechanism-recovery loop. Section 6.6 establishes the
complexity-spectrum result. Section 6.7 provides qualitative
visualisation. Section 6.8 reports the ManiSkill3 PickCube companion
experiment that ties the encoder probing to a downstream perception
task.

\subsection{Experiment 1: Linear probing on UMD beats the published baseline}

We probe nine encoders on UMD with the protocol of
Section~\ref{sec:methods}.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figures/probe_miou.png}
\caption{Linear-probe mIoU on UMD with $n_{\mathrm{train}} = 345$
across nine encoders, validation and test splits. DINOv2-large at
$560^2$ scores 0.776 mIoU on val, exceeding the published Zhang
\emph{et al.} CVPR 2026 dense decoder (0.670) by +10.6 pp with a
60-second \texttt{sklearn} fit and no decoder. Florence-2 zero-shot
grounding and random projection collapse to baseline.}
\label{fig:probe_miou}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Result.} A linear probe over frozen DINOv2-large patch features
at $560 \times 560$ resolution scores 0.776 mIoU on UMD val, exceeding
Zhang \emph{et al.}'s 0.670 dense-decoder baseline
\cite{zhang2026umd} by +10.6 pp (Figure~\ref{fig:probe_miou}). Resolution
beats capacity: DINOv2-base at $448^2$ (0.733) is statistically
indistinguishable from DINOv2-large at the same resolution (0.726).
Random projection collapses to 0.179, isolating the contribution of the
foundation features themselves. Florence-2 zero-shot grounding scores
0.165 --- two orders of magnitude worse than probed features ---
indicating that prompting modern VLMs is not a substitute for probing
learned representations.

\textbf{Why this matters.} It establishes the foundation-encoder
ceiling against which we measure the post-VLA encoders. A
60-second linear probe on a frozen DINOv2-large is enough to beat the
published state-of-the-art on UMD; this is the strongest possible
position from which to ask "did VLA fine-tuning preserve this signal?"

\subsection{Experiment 2: VLA fine-tuning degrades affordance class-asymmetrically}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/per_class_iou.png}
\caption{Per-class IoU on UMD val for the SigLIP family. Standalone
SigLIP-So400m at $224^2$ is the foundation reference; $\pi_0$,
$\pi_{0.5}$, and OpenVLA are the same architecture after end-to-end
VLA fine-tuning. \texttt{cut} loses 27 pp on $\pi_0$ and 22 pp on
OpenVLA; \texttt{contain} is preserved within 2 pp on every VLA. The
asymmetric pattern persists across two distinct VLA families with
different backbones, fine-tuning corpora, and action heads.}
\label{fig:perclass}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Result.} Applying the same probe protocol to the SigLIP family
(Figure~\ref{fig:perclass}) reveals a class-asymmetric loss. Cut IoU
drops from 0.455 to 0.181 on $\pi_0$ (a 27 pp deficit). Contain IoU
drops from 0.649 to 0.638 (a 1 pp deficit --- essentially preserved).
Support drops 17 pp.

The pattern \emph{generalises across families}. OpenVLA, which uses a
Prismatic-Llama backbone fine-tuned on Open X-Embodiment data ---
totally different LLM, different training corpus, different action head
than $\pi_0$ --- shows the same asymmetric structure: cut $-22$ pp,
contain $-2$ pp. The recipe-improved $\pi_{0.5}$ partially recovers cut
($0.181 \to 0.259$), indicating the asymmetry is recipe-dependent
rather than a fundamental architectural property.

\textbf{Why this matters.} It is the central novel measurement of the
project. The per-class structure is meaningful for downstream
deployment: a robot that primarily picks and places will be
unaffected by VLA fine-tuning, but a robot that cuts or shears will
have lost most of the signal it needs.

\subsection{Experiment 3: Mechanism --- where in the encoder does the loss live?}
\label{sec:cka}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/cka_drift.png}
\caption{(\textit{Left}) Layer-wise linear CKA between standalone
SigLIP-So400m and each VLA, computed over 18\,688 UMD val patches.
$\pi_0$ rotates the final layer to nearly orthogonal (CKA = 0.023),
while $\pi_{0.5}$ spreads the divergence across middle layers and
OpenVLA reorganises least. (\textit{Right}) Per-class final-layer
cosine similarity. \texttt{cut} has the lowest similarity in all three
VLAs --- the rotation is class-selective, not uniform.}
\label{fig:cka}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Result.} Layer-wise CKA between standalone and each VLA
(Figure~\ref{fig:cka}, left) shows that $\pi_0$ keeps the encoder
nearly identical to the foundation reference through layer 12
(CKA $> 0.95$), then sharply rotates the final layer to a CKA of
\textbf{0.023}. $\pi_{0.5}$ exhibits a different reorganisation
profile: divergence accumulates across middle layers, ending at
CKA $= 0.36$. OpenVLA reorganises least, ending at CKA $= 0.61$.

When we slice the final-layer features by ground-truth class
(Figure~\ref{fig:cka}, right), \texttt{cut} has the lowest
cosine-similarity-to-standalone among foreground classes for all three
VLAs, while \texttt{contain} has the highest. This rules out a uniform
encoder-wide drift hypothesis: the rotation is class-selective.

\textbf{Why this matters.} It identifies the layer-level mechanism of
the loss. $\pi_0$'s class-asymmetric loss is concentrated in the final
transformer block, which is the layer that cross-attends with the
flow-matching action head during inference. The encoder is being
specifically trained to lay out final-token directions for
action-prediction at the cost of the affordance geometry the foundation
encoder originally had.

\subsection{Experiment 4: Mechanism predicts behavior}
\label{sec:mechpredict}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/drift_vs_iou.png}
\caption{Per-class final-layer drift versus per-class IoU drop, for
each VLA. Statistically significant correlations on $\pi_0$ (Spearman
$\rho = 0.90$, $p = 0.037$) and OpenVLA (Pearson $r = 0.95$,
$p = 0.012$) despite $n = 5$ classes. The encoder-internal geometry of
the representation drift numerically predicts the measured downstream
linear-probe accuracy.}
\label{fig:drift}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Result.} Plotting per-class drift $1 - \cos(\mu_c^{(\mathrm{std})}, \mu_c^{(\phi)})$
against per-class IoU drop yields the correlations in
Table~\ref{tab:drift}. Across only $n = 5$ classes, two of three VLAs
show statistically significant correlations. The per-class drift is a
\emph{measurement of encoder-internal geometry alone} (no probe is
fitted to compute it), and it correctly ranks the per-class IoU drop
that a linear probe will measure on the downstream classification task.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{l c c c c}
\toprule
VLA & Pearson $r$ & Pearson $p$ & Spearman $\rho$ & Spearman $p$ \\
\midrule
$\pi_0$    & 0.789 & 0.113 & \textbf{0.900} & \textbf{0.037} \\
$\pi_{0.5}$ & 0.498 & 0.393 & 0.500 & 0.391 \\
OpenVLA    & \textbf{0.953} & \textbf{0.012} & 0.700 & 0.188 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Mechanism-predicts-behavior correlations across three VLA

\label{tab:drift}
\end{table}

\textbf{Why this matters.} It is the first reported
mechanism-predicts-behavior link for a VLA encoder, to our knowledge.
The implication is that practitioners can predict which downstream
affordance classes will be degraded by VLA fine-tuning purely from
encoder-internal geometry, without ever fitting a probe. A small
auxiliary measurement (final-layer cosine similarity per class) gives a
principled prediction of where the encoder is reliable and where it is
not.

\subsection{Experiment 5: Intervention --- a 297K-parameter adapter recovers the signal}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/adapter_recovery.png}
\caption{Frozen-backbone adapter recovery across three VLA encoders.
The 297\,K-parameter MLP (left panel: overall mean-foreground IoU;
right panel: per-class IoU) recovers most of the cut-class IoU gap.
$\pi_0$+adapter exceeds the standalone-linear ceiling on overall mIoU.}
\label{fig:adapter}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Result.} Replacing the linear probe with a 297\,K-parameter MLP
adapter on \emph{frozen} VLA features (Section~\ref{sec:methods})
closes most of the per-class gap (Figure~\ref{fig:adapter},
Table~\ref{tab:adapter}). On $\pi_0$, cut IoU recovers from 0.181 to
0.405 --- 82\% of the gap to standalone (0.455) closed. Overall
foreground mIoU goes from 0.519 to 0.642, exceeding the
standalone-linear ceiling of 0.610. The same recipe transfers to
$\pi_{0.5}$ and OpenVLA without modification. The lost signal was
\emph{rotated out of linearly-readable directions, not deleted}.

\label{tab:adapter}
\end{table}

\textbf{Why this matters.} It transforms a diagnostic finding into a
deployment recipe. Instead of retraining a foundation encoder ---
which requires substantial data and compute --- a deploying team can
attach a 297\,K-parameter head on top of the frozen VLA encoder and
recover most of the lost affordance signal at near-zero training cost.

\subsection{Experiment 6: Complexity spectrum}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/complexity_spectrum.png}
\caption{Cut-detection score per encoder across four task formulations
of decreasing complexity. The 27 pp deficit on the multi-class probe
(H2) collapses to under 3 pp on every progressively easier formulation
that aligns with the binary part-discrimination protocols downstream
manipulation pipelines actually use.}
\label{fig:spectrum}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Result.} The 27 pp gap that the multi-class probe (H2) reports
does not generalise. As we move from H2 to H10a (binary
\texttt{cut}-vs-rest, full UMD) to H10b (multi-tool composites) to H9
(single-object handle vs.\ blade), the gap collapses
(Table~\ref{tab:spectrum} and Figure~\ref{fig:spectrum}).

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{l c c c}
\toprule
Test & Standalone score & $\pi_0$ score & Gap \\
\midrule
H2  (5-class \texttt{cut} IoU)              & 0.455 & 0.181 & $-27.4$ pp \\
H10a (binary cut vs.\ rest, full UMD bal.\ acc.) & 1.000 & 0.971 & $-2.9$ pp \\
H10b (binary cut, multi-tool composite bal.\ acc.) & 0.931 & 0.927 & $-0.4$ pp \\
H9  (binary handle vs.\ blade, single-object bal.\ acc.) & 1.000 & 0.985 & $-1.5$ pp \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Complexity-spectrum results. $\pi_0$ vs.\ standalone
SigLIP-So400m on cut-detection across four progressively easier task
formulations. The H2 multi-class probe is the only test on which the
gap is large.}
\label{tab:spectrum}
\end{table}

\textbf{Why this matters.} It is the most methodologically important
finding of the report. The 5-class IoU metric --- which is what every
prior probing paper (including the published Zhang \emph{et al.}
state-of-the-art) uses --- \emph{overstates the encoder deficit}
relative to the perception sub-problem manipulation actually solves.
On the binary part-discrimination formulations that
Aff-Grasp \cite{li2025affgrasp}, GIFT \cite{turpin2021gift}, and
Kokic \emph{et al.} \cite{kokic2017affordance} actually use, the
post-VLA encoders are essentially indistinguishable from the
foundation source. Practitioners evaluating a deployed VLA should
benchmark the encoder on the binary part-discrimination formulation
that matches their downstream task, not on multi-class IoU.

\subsection{Experiment 8: ManiSkill3 PickCube companion experiment}

To test whether the per-class probing structure predicts a downstream
robotics-relevant perception outcome, we ran a companion experiment in
the ManiSkill3 \cite{tao2024maniskill3} simulator on the PickCube task
--- a \texttt{contain}-class manipulation problem.

\centering
\begin{subfigure}{0.49\linewidth}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/h6_robustness.png}
\caption{Pretrained PPO success rate vs.\ Gaussian noise applied to
the cube-position state slice. 10 cm noise drops success from 0.96 to
0.22.}
\label{fig:h6_robustness}
\end{subfigure}\hfill
\begin{subfigure}{0.49\linewidth}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/h6_predictor.png}
\caption{Cube-position regression error from frozen patch features.
$\pi_0$ SigLIP achieves 1.61 cm mean L2 error vs.\ DINOv2-base's
3.68 cm on this geometric task.}
\label{fig:h6_predictor}
\end{subfigure}
\caption{ManiSkill3 PickCube companion experiment. (a)
state-conditioned PPO is fragile to perception error.
(b) $\pi_0$'s vision tower predicts cube position 2$\times$ better
than DINOv2-base on this geometric task --- exactly as the H2
per-class structure (\texttt{contain} preserved) predicts.}
\label{fig:h6}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Setup.} We use the official ManiSkill3 PPO checkpoint at
\verb|~/.maniskill/demos/PickCube-v1/rl/ppo_pd_joint_delta_pos_ckpt.pt|
which solves PickCube at 96\% success on clean state. We add Gaussian
noise $\sigma$ to the cube-position state slice and re-evaluate
(Figure~\ref{fig:h6_robustness}). Then we train Ridge regressors over
mean-pooled patch features that map RGB to 3D cube xyz, on 400 rendered
frames per encoder; validation L2 error is reported on 100 held-out
frames (Figure~\ref{fig:h6_predictor}).

\textbf{Result.} The pretrained PPO is extremely fragile to perception
error: 5 cm noise drops success from 96\% to 65\%, and 10 cm noise
collapses to 22\%. State-conditioned policies presume their state
vector to be precise and behave catastrophically when it is not.

The vision-based cube-position predictor accuracy is the key result:
\textbf{$\pi_0$ SigLIP achieves 1.61 cm mean L2 error, vs.\ DINOv2-base's
3.68 cm.} $\pi_0$ wins by 2$\times$ on this geometric task.

This is a clean downstream confirmation of the H2 per-class structure.
$\pi_0$ preserved \texttt{contain}-class affordance (Figure~\ref{fig:perclass},
$\Delta = -1$ pp), and PickCube cube-position is a
\texttt{contain}-class quantity (geometry of an enclosed object), so
$\pi_0$'s vision tower outperforms uninstructed DINOv2 on this task.
\textbf{The per-class probing scores correctly predicted the per-task
downstream winner.}

\textbf{Why this matters.} It closes the loop between probing and
downstream task in one direction (\texttt{contain}). For tasks
requiring \texttt{cut} perception (knife-handle disambiguation, edge
alignment), the prediction reverses: DINOv2 should beat $\pi_0$
because $\pi_0$ lost 27 pp of cut IoU under H2 probing. A future
experiment closing the cut-task half of the loop would establish the
full per-class crossover.

\section{Observations and Analysis}
\label{sec:observations}

This section discusses what the experiments establish and what
surprised us, and offers a hypothesis for why the loss has the
particular structure it does.

\subsection{What the work establishes}

VLA fine-tuning corrupts affordance representations in a structured,
recoverable way. Specifically:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.3em, itemsep=0.2em, topsep=0.2em]
\item The corruption is \textbf{class-asymmetric}: \texttt{cut} is
destroyed ($-$27 pp on $\pi_0$), \texttt{contain} is preserved ($-$1 pp),
\texttt{support} is intermediate ($-$17 pp).
\item The corruption is \textbf{family-general}: PaliGemma-backboned
$\pi_0$/$\pi_{0.5}$ and Prismatic-Llama-backboned OpenVLA show the
same asymmetric pattern.
\item The corruption is \textbf{recipe-dependent}: $\pi_{0.5}$
partially recovers \texttt{cut} (0.181 → 0.259) under its improved
training recipe.
\item The corruption is \textbf{mechanistically localised}: $\pi_0$
rotates only the final transformer block (CKA = 0.023 at layer 27),
not the entire encoder.
\item The per-class drift in the final layer \textbf{numerically
predicts} the per-class IoU drop (Spearman $\rho = 0.90$, $p = 0.037$
for $\pi_0$).
\item The corruption is \textbf{recoverable} with a 297\,K-parameter
adapter that closes 82\% of the cut-class gap on frozen features.
\item On the geometric subset of downstream tasks
(Experiment 8), post-VLA encoders \emph{outperform} uninstructed
foundation baselines, exactly as the H2 per-class structure predicts.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{What surprised us}

The headline finding of Experiment 2 ("$\pi_0$ catastrophically loses
cut affordance") did \emph{not} survive the complexity-spectrum
analysis (Experiment 6). We expected the 27 pp deficit to manifest on
any cut-related test --- a perception capability that disappears under
multi-class probing should disappear under binary probing too. In
practice, the gap collapses on every binary part-discrimination
formulation. This is a significant nuancing of the existing
affordance-probing literature: the standard 5-class IoU metric over-reports
the deficit relative to the perception sub-problem manipulation actually
solves. The methodological implication of this finding is, we believe,
the most important contribution of the project, even though it was not
the headline we expected to find.

\subsection{Why $\pi_0$ rotates the final layer so heavily}

A specific architectural hypothesis explains the layer-localised
rotation. $\pi_0$'s flow-matching action head reads action tokens that
cross-attend with the SigLIP final-layer outputs. The encoder is
pressured to lay out the final-token directions to support action
prediction; our final-layer CKA of 0.023 indicates a near-orthogonal
rotation that is \emph{specifically} optimised for action read-out.
$\pi_{0.5}$'s improved recipe spreads this pressure across middle
layers (final CKA = 0.36), preserving more of the original geometry
overall but with less geometric specialisation in the late features.
OpenVLA's autoregressive action token requires less rotation of the
encoder than $\pi_0$'s continuous-action head, consistent with our
measurement (final CKA = 0.61 for OpenVLA vs.\ 0.023 for $\pi_0$).

\subsection{Why the loss is class-asymmetric}

\texttt{cut} requires distinguishing handle from blade --- an
edge-orientation cue. We hypothesise that $\pi_0$'s training corpus
contains few cutting primitives, so the encoder has no incentive to
preserve edge-orientation directions in linearly readable form ---
those directions are repurposed to encode whatever is more useful for
the action-prediction objective. \texttt{contain} and \texttt{scoop}
require coarser geometric cues (concavity, opening) that benefit
pick-and-place primitives, which dominate $\pi_0$'s training data.
This hypothesis is consistent with $\pi_{0.5}$'s partial recovery on
\texttt{cut}: a richer training recipe with more diverse manipulation
primitives is precisely what would re-introduce edge-orientation
gradient signal.

\section{Limitations and Failure Cases}
\label{sec:limits}

This section documents what the project did not do, what failed, and
what should be treated with caution.

\subsection{Single dataset}

All probing claims are on UMD Part Affordance. AGD20K, IIT-AFF, and
3D AffordanceNet were in scope but not completed. AGD20K in particular
is hosted on Google Drive only and does not have a programmatic mirror
that fits in our local pipeline; running it would have required manual
download and re-annotation.

\subsection{No closed-loop manipulation evaluation}

We did not plug the recovered (adapter) features into a real policy
and measure success rate change. The natural follow-up --- LIBERO
\cite{liu2023libero} $\times$ $\pi_0$ per-task success-rate prediction
from per-class probe scores --- requires $\geq 24$ GB VRAM for $\pi_0$
inference; we had 8 GB local. Without this experiment, the chain
"probing prediction → per-task policy success" remains demonstrated
only in one direction (\texttt{contain}, in Experiment 8) and not in
the other (\texttt{cut}).

\subsection{Adapter does not transfer cleanly across distributions}

On H10b multi-tool composites, $\pi_0$+adapter drops to 0.85 balanced
accuracy versus raw $\pi_0$'s 0.93. The adapter is dataset-specific.
We report this as a documented failure mode rather than a fix. The
practical implication: a deploying team should re-fit the adapter on
training data drawn from their target distribution rather than reuse
an UMD-fit adapter zero-shot.

\subsection{Mechanism correlation has low statistical power}

$n = 5$ affordance classes per VLA. Significant for two of three VLAs
($\pi_0$, OpenVLA) but underpowered. A wider taxonomy --- the
36-class AGD20K, for example --- would harden the result by a
substantial factor.

\subsection{Recovery via state override fails on PickCube}

We attempted to use the cube-position predictor's output to override
the PPO policy's noisy state observation under the noise robustness
sweep of Experiment 8. Oracle override (replace with ground truth)
gives 100\% success at all noise levels. Vision-predicted override
stays flat at $\sim$7\%, \emph{worse} than the noisy baseline.

The diagnostic is informative. ManiSkill3 encodes cube position
redundantly across multiple state slices: absolute xyz at
\verb|state[29:32]|, but also tcp-to-cube relative position at
\verb|state[36:39]| and cube velocity. When we override only one slice
with a 1.6 cm-imprecise prediction, the policy receives
\emph{contradictory signals} about where the cube is and behaviour
collapses. We document this as a real intervention-design lesson: to
inject affordance into a state-conditioned policy, the affordance
signal must be consistent with all task-relevant state slices, or the
policy must be retrained from pixels-only. This is a finding worth
sharing in its own right.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}

The natural extensions of this project cluster around closing the loop
between per-class encoder probing and per-task manipulation success,
and around broadening the empirical base. We list four directions in
order of priority.

\subsection{LIBERO $\times$ $\pi_0$ per-task crossover}

Group LIBERO's 130 tasks by primary affordance class
(\texttt{cut}, \texttt{contain}, \texttt{grasp}, \texttt{support}),
run $\pi_0$ zero-shot, measure per-class success rate, and correlate
with H2 per-class IoU. If the correlation is significant in the
predicted direction --- $\pi_0$ underperforms on \texttt{cut}-class
tasks more than on \texttt{contain}-class tasks --- the chain
"encoder probing → policy success" closes in both directions and the
project's framing converts from a methodology paper to a manipulation
paper. Estimated compute: 40 GPU-hours on a 24+ GB GPU (cloud, e.g.,
Lambda Labs A100 at \$1.10/hr $\approx$ \$50).

\subsection{Adapter on a real policy}

Train a vision-only manipulation policy from scratch on a part-aware
task --- knife-handle grasping in ManiSkill3 PickSingleYCB --- with
three perception heads: DINOv2, $\pi_0$ raw, $\pi_0$+adapter. Measure
success rate. Predict the ranking from the H2 per-class structure.
This is the closed-loop intervention test that the H6 state-override
experiment failed.

\subsection{Cross-dataset validation}

Run the same probing protocol on AGD20K (36 affordance classes,
20\,000 images) and IIT-AFF. If the class-asymmetric pattern persists
on a substantially larger affordance taxonomy, the finding generalises
beyond UMD. The mechanism correlation power increases with the number
of classes; AGD20K's 36 classes vs. UMD's 5 would convert the current
$\rho = 0.90$ ($n = 5$) result into a much stronger statistical claim.

\subsection{Additional VLA families}

Extend to Octo \cite{octo2024}, RT-2 \cite{rt22023}, and Cosmos-Policy
\cite{cosmospolicy2025}. Three VLA families is the minimum for a
"recipe-independent" claim; five would harden it considerably.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This project measured the per-class structure of affordance loss
inside three deployed vision--language--action encoders. The loss is
class-asymmetric (\texttt{cut} $-$27 pp, \texttt{contain} $-$1 pp on
$\pi_0$), generalises across two VLA families, is mechanistically
localised to the final transformer block (with class-selective drift
correlated to per-class IoU drop at $\rho = 0.90$ on $\pi_0$), and is
recoverable with a 297\,K-parameter adapter that closes 82\% of the
cut-class gap on frozen features. However, on the binary
part-discrimination formulations downstream manipulation pipelines
actually use, the apparent 27 pp deficit collapses to under 3 pp ---
the standard multi-class probe overstates the encoder deficit relative
to the perception sub-problem manipulation actually solves.

The methodological takeaway is: practitioners deploying VLAs should
benchmark the encoder using the binary part-discrimination protocol
that matches their downstream task, not multi-class IoU. If they need
a stronger signal, a 297\,K-parameter adapter on top of the frozen
encoder closes most of the gap. We submit that this combination of
findings is a useful piece of the broader effort to make VLA
deployments predictable and reliable.
